Pediatric Cancer & the Education System

The transition back to school can be hard for parents to undergo, especially without guidance. There are many different things to consider and questions that need to be addressed directly between a child’s parents or guardian and the child’s teachers and school administrators. All of these concerns once addressed can help make parents and families know their child will be properly cared for and educated while at school. Since a child’s cancer diagnosis and treatment can interrupt their regular school routines, informing school educators and administrators of how to best care for a student who is recovering from this can have a great effect on a child’s social and academic development after returning to school. It can be comforting as a parent to know that going back to school will help to put their child in the best environment for them to succeed.

These sections will guide parents and guardians as well as educators through the main steps they need to complete to plan for their child to return to school and some topics of concern to address with educators and school administrators to ensure their child has the right support at school.

Information for Parents

What the school should know:

Education

Educational Needs. The school needs to have documents for any Individualized Education plans (IEP) or 504 plan if the child needs assistance learning

Schoolwork and Assignments. It is important to communicate with the teacher to see if the child’s school work and notes for class can be provided electronically as well in the case the child needs to be absent for any reason.

Health

Health Monitoring Devices. In the case of using devices that monitor a child’s health, a child’s educator or school nurse may need to be responsible for monitoring a child’s health conditions while in class or during the school day. They will need to be notified of what apps or technology they need to download.

Medications. A child’s school nurse will need to know any medications they need to take during the school hours and how they should be administered. It may also be helpful to have the contact information of the child’s pediatrician and nurse on file with the school’s nurse as well.

Accommodations

Fitness Activity Restrictions. There may be activities your child cannot participate in that may impact their health (ex. extended hours outside during recess, running laps in the gym or track). The child’s school may need written documentation to excuse the child from these activities especially if they are required as part of the child’s classroom or physical education.

Special devices. The child may need to assist them during the class and how to use them (ex. hearing aids) as well as special accommodations needed (ex. wheelchair, walkers, or needs for other special devices).

Lunch accommodations. For children who may have special eating requirements and may need to eat more meals or snacks during the school day outside of the school’s normal lunch and breakfast hours

Bathroom. Children who have treatments that affect their bodily functions, may need additional bathroom breaks since they have more frequent visits to the bathroom than are allowed within a given time frame at their school.

Emergencies

Emergency procedures. Actions that need to be taken and performed if possible problems occur.

Special precautions. Educators and school administrators will need to know if there are actions that need to be taken in certain situations that may arise during the school day. For instance, if the child has a weakened immune system, and other children in the class are sick or feverish they will need to know what to do in this situation.

Emergency Contacts. The school nurse and administrators would need access to who to contact in case of emergency or to answer questions the school may have. This includes a child’s primary care providers and nurses.

Information for Educators

What to discuss with parents and guardians:

Attendance. A student may miss days or weeks of classes for treatment and appointments, and there should be an arrangement so that these absences can be properly addressed and excused and how a child can recover missed work. The school will need to provide their own absence forms for medical leave for parents to complete on the behalf of students or provide a doctor’s note.

Lunch Accommodations. A child may need to be allowed to eat outside of normal lunch and snack time to adhere to their medication, so a student’s teacher may need to be informed of when and where a child is able to eat their lunch and snacks.

Dress code. Some students may want permission to wear hats or scarves in school during their treatment if it causes their hair to fall out and they prefer to keep their head covered and this may not be allowed as part of the school’s dress code

Classroom expectations and requirements. If a student has entered into a grade or class where there are certain aspects or assignments that they cannot physically participate in such as gym class and other elective classes

School schedule. A student may need to take fewer classes or change their schedule for treatment; school staff may need to contact the hospital or discuss this with the parent to see if there is an education coordinator that can assist in the planning for this.

Digital class material. Teachers may also need to have additional copies of notes, recordings of lectures, and homework available electronically for students so that it is easy for teachers to send parents and students work a child may have missed in a digital format.
